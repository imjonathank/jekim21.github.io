About me

I'm currently a joint BA/MA student at the University of Chicago in Chicago, Illinois.

As a Classics major and a Digital Studies MA candidate, my academic interests center around the study of religion (primarily ancient) and using digital technology to understand our linguistic and cultural relationships to religious institutions. My undergraduate thesis focuses on the reception of non-Greco-Roman deities in Apulieus' Metamorphoses. As a MA candidate, I would like to more broadly understand religious conversions: what causes some to 'convert' to a particular religion, how people/society depend on religious ideas to understand relationships (both contemporaneously and historically), and examination of linguistic trends to describe their beliefs.

About Apuleius' Metamorphoses

Apuleius' Metamorphoses

(also known as Asinus Aureus) stands out among Latin literature as the sole surviving ancient Roman novel. The story follows a young man named Lucius, who through a series of unfortunate magical events, is turned into a donkey. His efforts to turn himself back into a human lead him across the Roman Empire, encountering a wide variety of people and religions throughout his journey. Most significantly, Lucius undergoes both a physical and spiritual metamorphosis: he is ultimately saved during a festival to the Egyptian goddess Isis, to whom he now pledges cultic devotion. The Metamorphoses, then, is as much an in-depth examination of a young man (a Latin bildungsroman of sorts) as it is an assessment of broader Roman religion.
